12.1 Introduction

The BLISS-RS framework is discipline-agnostic: the four-discipline supersonic business jet was chosen as the original test case because of the availability of validated analysis codes and a documented optimum, but the algorithm applies equally to any engineering system that can be decomposed into coupled subsystems.

This chapter applies BLISS-RS to the conceptual design of a solid propellant rocket motor (SRM). Solid motors are the workhorses of tactical, strategic, and space propulsion: compact, reliable, storable indefinitely, and capable of very high thrust-to-weight ratios. Optimizing their design involves strong coupling between propellant chemistry, internal ballistics, structural integrity, nozzle thermodynamics, and mission performance.

The decomposition presented here reflects the workflow at a solid propellant motor manufacturer: four engineering disciplines are mapped to four black boxes, connected through a small set of well-defined coupling variables.

12.2 Physical Background

12.2.1 Saint-Robert’s Burn Rate Law

The burn rate of a solid propellant follows Saint-Robert’s (Vieille’s) empirical law:

\[ r = a \cdot P_c^n \tag{12.1}\]

where \(r\) [in/s] is the linear regression rate of the propellant surface, \(P_c\) [psia] is the chamber pressure, \(a\) is the burn rate coefficient, and \(n\) is the pressure exponent. For AP/Al/HTPB composites, typical values are:

Property Range
\(a\) (burn rate coefficient) 0.030–0.050 in/s·psia\(^{-n}\)
\(n\) (pressure exponent) 0.30–0.45
\(T_c\) (flame temperature) 5200–5800 °R
\(\gamma\) (specific heat ratio) 1.18–1.24
\(c^*\) (characteristic velocity) 4800–5200 ft/s

12.2.2 Chamber Pressure Balance

In quasi-steady operation, the mass flow rate produced by propellant combustion must equal the mass flow rate through the nozzle throat:

\[ \dot{m}_{produced} = \rho_p \cdot A_b \cdot r = \rho_p \cdot A_b \cdot a \cdot P_c^n \]

\[ \dot{m}_{expelled} = \frac{P_c \cdot A_t}{c^*} \]

Setting these equal and solving for the equilibrium chamber pressure:

\[ P_c = \left(\frac{\rho_p \cdot a \cdot A_b \cdot c^*}{A_t}\right)^{1/(1-n)} \tag{12.2}\]

This is the fundamental design equation for an SRM: chamber pressure is determined by the ratio of burning surface area \(A_b\) to throat area \(A_t\), the propellant properties (\(\rho_p\), \(a\), \(c^*\)), and the pressure exponent \(n\).

12.2.3 Breguet Analogue: Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation

For mission performance, the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation plays the role of the Breguet range equation:

\[ \Delta V = I_{sp} \cdot g_0 \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{W_i}{W_f}\right) \tag{12.3}\]

where \(I_{sp}\) is the specific impulse [seconds], \(g_0 = 32.174\) ft/s², and \(W_i / W_f\) is the initial-to-final weight ratio.

12.6 BLISS-RS Optimization of the SRM

The SRM BLISS-RS optimization uses the same framework as the supersonic business jet but with the SRM black boxes.

Code 
using Optim

# System variable bounds: [L_D, Pc_target, epsilon, Phi_g]
Z_lo_srm = [3.0, 600.0,  4.0, 0.70]
Z_hi_srm = [8.0, 1500.0, 16.0, 0.93]
Z_init_srm = Z_srm

n_Z_srm = 4

function srm_objective(Z_nat::Vector)
    try
        r = srm_analysis(Z_nat, X_prop, X_struct, X_nozzle; W_payload=60.0)
        # Penalise constraint violations
        penalty = 0.0
        # Must have FS_burst > 1.5 (safety factor)
        penalty += max(0.0, 1.5 - r.FS_burst) * 2000.0
        # Pressure must be achievable (Pc within ±10% of target)
        penalty += abs(r.Pc - Z_nat[2]) / Z_nat[2] * 1000.0
        return r.delta_V - penalty
    catch
        return 0.0
    end
end

function run_srm_blissrs(n_iter=10, K=0.8; seed=42)
    bounds = hcat(Z_lo_srm, Z_hi_srm)
    Z_hist   = Vector{Vector{Float64}}()
    obj_hist = Float64[]
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)

    for it in 1:n_iter
        n_pts = max(min_rs_points(n_Z_srm) + 6, 25)
        pts_c = d_optimal_points(n_Z_srm, n_pts; seed=seed + it)
        pts_n = similar(pts_c)
        for j in 1:n_Z_srm
            pts_n[:, j] = bounds[j,1] .+ pts_c[:, j] .* (bounds[j,2] - bounds[j,1])
        end

        y_vals = map(1:n_pts) do i
            srm_objective(pts_n[i, :])
        end

        b = fit_rs(pts_c, y_vals)

        # Maximise RS
        neg_rs(ξ) = -only(predict_rs(reshape(ξ, 1, :), b))
        result = Optim.optimize(neg_rs, fill(0.5, n_Z_srm),
                                 NelderMead(),
                                 Optim.Options(iterations=3000, show_trace=false))
        ξ_opt = clamp.(Optim.minimizer(result), 0.0, 1.0)
        Z_opt = [bounds[j,1] + ξ_opt[j]*(bounds[j,2]-bounds[j,1]) for j in 1:n_Z_srm]
        obj_opt = srm_objective(Z_opt)

        push!(Z_hist, Z_opt)
        push!(obj_hist, obj_opt)

        bounds = update_intervals(ξ_opt, bounds; K=K)
    end
    return Z_hist, obj_hist
end

Z_srm_hist, obj_srm_hist = run_srm_blissrs(10, 0.8; seed=42)
println("Done. Final ΔV: $(round(obj_srm_hist[end], digits=0)) ft/s")
Done. Final ΔV: 1312.0 ft/s

12.9 Connection to Physical Testing and Backfitting

The SRM analysis modules presented here implement predictive internal ballistics — computing \(P_c(t)\) and \(F(t)\) from propellant properties and grain geometry. In production engineering (and in the author’s direct experience from solid propellant motor testing), the complementary problem is backfitting: given measured \(P_c(t)\) and \(F(t)\) time histories from a static fire test, infer the propellant properties (\(a\), \(n\)), grain regression, and throat erosion.

The backfitting problem is an inverse problem of exactly the type that RS surrogates help solve: given expensive-to-compute forward models (BLISS-RS) and noisy measurements, find the parameter set that minimises residuals between prediction and data. Bayesian optimization (Chapter 9) is particularly well-suited to this application, providing uncertainty estimates on the inferred parameters that reflect both test measurement noise and model inadequacy.

12.9.1 Temperature Sensitivity Correction

A critical concern in tactical motor qualification is temperature sensitivity: solid propellants exhibit substantially different \(P_c(t)\) and \(F(t)\) profiles at hot, nominal, and cold storage temperatures (typical range: −65°F to +165°F for military systems). The burn rate coefficient \(a\) is temperature-dependent:

\[ a(T) = a_{\text{ref}} \cdot \exp[\sigma_p (T - T_{\text{ref}})] \tag{12.4}\]

where \(\sigma_p\) [°F\(^{-1}\)] is the temperature sensitivity coefficient. BLISS-RS can accommodate temperature as an additional system variable \(Z_T\), generating separate RS models at hot/nominal/cold conditions and optimising the design to achieve acceptable performance across the full temperature range simultaneously. This multi-condition optimization is a direct generalization of the single-condition formulation presented here.

Code 
# Simulate P_c(t) / F(t) at 3 temperatures
# Temperature sensitivity: a(T) = a_ref * exp(σp * (T - T_ref))
sigma_p = 0.001   # typical AP/HTPB value [1/°F]
T_ref   = 70.0    # reference temperature [°F]
temps   = [-65.0, 70.0, 165.0]
labels_ = ["-65°F (Cold)", "70°F (Nominal)", "165°F (Hot)"]
colors_ = [:steelblue, :black, :crimson]

t_range = range(0.0, 25.0, length=300)

p = plot(title="SRM Thrust vs. Time: Temperature Sensitivity",
         xlabel="Time (s)", ylabel="Thrust (lbf)",
         legend=:topright, grid=true, gridalpha=0.3)

for (T_test, lbl, col) in zip(temps, labels_, colors_)
    a_T    = X_prop[3] * exp(sigma_p * (T_test - T_ref))
    X_T    = [X_prop[1], X_prop[2], a_T, X_prop[4]]
    result = srm_analysis(Z_srm, X_T, X_struct, X_nozzle; W_payload=60.0)
    t_burn = result.t_burn

    # Simple rectangular approximation of thrust profile
    F_vals = [t_val <= t_burn ? result.F : 0.0 for t_val in t_range]
    plot!(t_range, F_vals, lw=2, color=col, label=lbl)
end

12.10 Design Insights and Engineering Interpretation

The BLISS-RS SRM optimization reveals several physically meaningful trends:

  1. Expansion ratio is the highest-leverage system variable for \(\Delta V\): increasing \(\varepsilon\) from 8 to ~12–14 improves vacuum \(I_{sp}\) significantly at moderate additional nozzle weight.

  2. Volumetric loading should be pushed as high as the grain geometry allows (subject to structural limits and adequate burning-surface regression), because propellant mass fraction directly enters the Tsiolkovsky equation logarithmically.

  3. Chamber pressure has diminishing returns above ~1000 psia for AP/Al/HTPB propellants: \(c^*\) is weakly dependent on \(P_c\) in this range, while structural weight grows with \(P_c\), eroding the mass fraction benefit.

  4. L/D ratio primarily affects slenderness and burn time; for a BATES grain, a shorter, fatter motor (lower L/D) increases burning surface for a given web thickness, enabling shorter burn time at higher thrust if so desired.

These insights mirror the design heuristics developed empirically at SRM manufacturers and are recovered here purely from the BLISS-RS optimization, validating the framework’s ability to capture physical design logic through the coupling of disciplinary analysis models.

## BLISS-RS Optimization of the SRM

The SRM BLISS-RS optimization uses the same framework as the supersonic business jet
but with the SRM black boxes.

```{julia}
#| label: srm-blissrs
#| code-fold: true

using Optim

# System variable bounds: [L_D, Pc_target, epsilon, Phi_g]
Z_lo_srm = [3.0, 600.0,  4.0, 0.70]
Z_hi_srm = [8.0, 1500.0, 16.0, 0.93]
Z_init_srm = Z_srm

n_Z_srm = 4

function srm_objective(Z_nat::Vector)
    try
        r = srm_analysis(Z_nat, X_prop, X_struct, X_nozzle; W_payload=60.0)
        # Penalise constraint violations
        penalty = 0.0
        # Must have FS_burst > 1.5 (safety factor)
        penalty += max(0.0, 1.5 - r.FS_burst) * 2000.0
        # Pressure must be achievable (Pc within ±10% of target)
        penalty += abs(r.Pc - Z_nat[2]) / Z_nat[2] * 1000.0
        return r.delta_V - penalty
    catch
        return 0.0
    end
end

function run_srm_blissrs(n_iter=10, K=0.8; seed=42)
    bounds = hcat(Z_lo_srm, Z_hi_srm)
    Z_hist   = Vector{Vector{Float64}}()
    obj_hist = Float64[]
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)

    for it in 1:n_iter
        n_pts = max(min_rs_points(n_Z_srm) + 6, 25)
        pts_c = d_optimal_points(n_Z_srm, n_pts; seed=seed + it)
        pts_n = similar(pts_c)
        for j in 1:n_Z_srm
            pts_n[:, j] = bounds[j,1] .+ pts_c[:, j] .* (bounds[j,2] - bounds[j,1])
        end

        y_vals = map(1:n_pts) do i
            srm_objective(pts_n[i, :])
        end

        b = fit_rs(pts_c, y_vals)

        # Maximise RS
        neg_rs(ξ) = -only(predict_rs(reshape(ξ, 1, :), b))
        result = Optim.optimize(neg_rs, fill(0.5, n_Z_srm),
                                 NelderMead(),
                                 Optim.Options(iterations=3000, show_trace=false))
        ξ_opt = clamp.(Optim.minimizer(result), 0.0, 1.0)
        Z_opt = [bounds[j,1] + ξ_opt[j]*(bounds[j,2]-bounds[j,1]) for j in 1:n_Z_srm]
        obj_opt = srm_objective(Z_opt)

        push!(Z_hist, Z_opt)
        push!(obj_hist, obj_opt)

        bounds = update_intervals(ξ_opt, bounds; K=K)
    end
    return Z_hist, obj_hist
end

Z_srm_hist, obj_srm_hist = run_srm_blissrs(10, 0.8; seed=42)
println("Done. Final ΔV: $(round(obj_srm_hist[end], digits=0)) ft/s")
```

## Connection to Physical Testing and Backfitting

The SRM analysis modules presented here implement *predictive* internal ballistics —
computing $P_c(t)$ and $F(t)$ from propellant properties and grain geometry. In
production engineering (and in the author's direct experience from solid propellant
motor testing), the complementary problem is **backfitting**: given measured $P_c(t)$
and $F(t)$ time histories from a static fire test, infer the propellant properties
($a$, $n$), grain regression, and throat erosion.

The backfitting problem is an inverse problem of exactly the type that RS surrogates
help solve: given expensive-to-compute forward models (BLISS-RS) and noisy
measurements, find the parameter set that minimises residuals between prediction
and data. Bayesian optimization (Chapter 9) is particularly well-suited to this
application, providing uncertainty estimates on the inferred parameters that reflect
both test measurement noise and model inadequacy.

### Temperature Sensitivity Correction

A critical concern in tactical motor qualification is **temperature sensitivity**:
solid propellants exhibit substantially different $P_c(t)$ and $F(t)$ profiles at
hot, nominal, and cold storage temperatures (typical range: −65°F to +165°F for
military systems). The burn rate coefficient $a$ is temperature-dependent:

$$
a(T) = a_{\text{ref}} \cdot \exp[\sigma_p (T - T_{\text{ref}})]
$$ {#eq-temp-sensitivity}

where $\sigma_p$ [°F$^{-1}$] is the temperature sensitivity coefficient.
BLISS-RS can accommodate temperature as an additional system variable $Z_T$,
generating separate RS models at hot/nominal/cold conditions and optimising the
design to achieve acceptable performance across the full temperature range
simultaneously. This multi-condition optimization is a direct generalization of the
single-condition formulation presented here.

```{julia}
#| label: fig-temp-sensitivity
#| fig-cap: "Effect of storage temperature on SRM thrust-time history. Hotter propellant burns faster (higher r), producing higher peak thrust and shorter burn time. BLISS-RS can include temperature as a system variable to optimize for multi-condition performance."
#| code-fold: true

# Simulate P_c(t) / F(t) at 3 temperatures
# Temperature sensitivity: a(T) = a_ref * exp(σp * (T - T_ref))
sigma_p = 0.001   # typical AP/HTPB value [1/°F]
T_ref   = 70.0    # reference temperature [°F]
temps   = [-65.0, 70.0, 165.0]
labels_ = ["-65°F (Cold)", "70°F (Nominal)", "165°F (Hot)"]
colors_ = [:steelblue, :black, :crimson]

t_range = range(0.0, 25.0, length=300)

p = plot(title="SRM Thrust vs. Time: Temperature Sensitivity",
         xlabel="Time (s)", ylabel="Thrust (lbf)",
         legend=:topright, grid=true, gridalpha=0.3)

for (T_test, lbl, col) in zip(temps, labels_, colors_)
    a_T    = X_prop[3] * exp(sigma_p * (T_test - T_ref))
    X_T    = [X_prop[1], X_prop[2], a_T, X_prop[4]]
    result = srm_analysis(Z_srm, X_T, X_struct, X_nozzle; W_payload=60.0)
    t_burn = result.t_burn

    # Simple rectangular approximation of thrust profile
    F_vals = [t_val <= t_burn ? result.F : 0.0 for t_val in t_range]
    plot!(t_range, F_vals, lw=2, color=col, label=lbl)
end

p
```

## Design Insights and Engineering Interpretation

The BLISS-RS SRM optimization reveals several physically meaningful trends:

1. **Expansion ratio** is the highest-leverage system variable for $\Delta V$:
   increasing $\varepsilon$ from 8 to ~12–14 improves vacuum $I_{sp}$ significantly
   at moderate additional nozzle weight.

2. **Volumetric loading** should be pushed as high as the grain geometry allows
   (subject to structural limits and adequate burning-surface regression), because
   propellant mass fraction directly enters the Tsiolkovsky equation logarithmically.

3. **Chamber pressure** has diminishing returns above ~1000 psia for AP/Al/HTPB
   propellants: $c^*$ is weakly dependent on $P_c$ in this range, while structural
   weight grows with $P_c$, eroding the mass fraction benefit.

4. **L/D ratio** primarily affects slenderness and burn time; for a BATES grain, a
   shorter, fatter motor (lower L/D) increases burning surface for a given web
   thickness, enabling shorter burn time at higher thrust if so desired.

These insights mirror the design heuristics developed empirically at SRM manufacturers
and are recovered here purely from the BLISS-RS optimization, validating the
framework's ability to capture physical design logic through the coupling of
disciplinary analysis models.
