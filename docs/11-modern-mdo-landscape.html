12.1 MDO Decomposition: A Taxonomy

BLISS is one of several decomposition architectures developed over the past four decades to manage the complexity of multidisciplinary system design. Figure 12.1 maps the family.

12.1.1 MDF, IDF, and SAND

MDF (Multidisciplinary Feasible) enforces disciplinary consistency at every system analysis call. Couplings are satisfied implicitly by a fixed-point (Gauss-Seidel) or Newton iteration. The system optimizer sees only converged analyses but pays the cost of full convergence each function evaluation.

IDF (Individual Discipline Feasible) introduces explicit coupling variables as optimization unknowns. Disciplines run independently without iteration, and coupling consistency is enforced by the outer optimizer as equality constraints. This enables parallelism at the expense of a larger (but well-structured) optimization problem.

SAND (Simultaneous Analysis and Design) treats all state and design variables as optimization variables simultaneously. The optimizer solves the governing equations and the design problem jointly. Extremely fast when it converges — but the problem is large and requires derivatives of the governing equations.

12.1.2 Collaborative Optimisation and ATC

Collaborative Optimisation (CO) [1] places a separate optimizer at each discipline level that minimises the deviation between local design variables and the system-level targets. The system-level optimizer drives coupling consistency. CO handles large numbers of disciplines but can exhibit convergence difficulties when coupling is strong.

Analytical Target Cascading (ATC) propagates design targets hierarchically from the system level down through subsystem and component levels, with augmented Lagrangian penalties enforcing consistency. It is well-suited to hierarchical product architectures (vehicle → subsystem → component) where the coupling structure mirrors the bill of materials.

12.1.3 Where BLISS Sits

BLISS is a two-level decomposition that inherits IDF’s independent disciplinary structure but adds the distinction between system-level (\(Z\)) and local (\(X\)) variables. The key innovation is that the system optimizer never calls the disciplines directly — it operates entirely on surrogate models. This makes BLISS uniquely suited to black-box disciplines: codes that cannot be differentiated or whose source is unavailable.

12.2 Multi-Fidelity Methods

The most impactful practical extension to BLISS-2026 is multi-fidelity modeling: using a hierarchy of analysis tools of differing fidelity and cost to accelerate convergence.

12.2.1 Kennedy-O’Hagan Co-Kriging

Kennedy and O’Hagan [2] proposed a principled Bayesian framework for combining models at multiple fidelity levels. For two levels (cheap \(f_L\) and expensive \(f_H\)):

\[ f_H(\mathbf{x}) = \rho \cdot f_L(\mathbf{x}) + \delta(\mathbf{x}) \tag{12.1}\]

where \(\rho\) is a regression scaling coefficient and \(\delta(\mathbf{x})\) is an independent GP capturing the discrepancy between the two fidelity levels. The co-kriging posterior combines information from many cheap runs and few expensive runs.

The co-kriging posterior mean and variance at a test point \(\mathbf{x}_*\) are:

\[ \mu_{CK}(\mathbf{x}_*) = \rho\,\mu_L(\mathbf{x}_*) + \mu_\delta(\mathbf{x}_*) \tag{12.2}\]

\[ \sigma^2_{CK}(\mathbf{x}_*) = \rho^2\,\sigma^2_L(\mathbf{x}_*) + \sigma^2_\delta(\mathbf{x}_*) \tag{12.3}\]

where \(\mu_L, \sigma^2_L\) are the low-fidelity GP posterior moments and \(\mu_\delta, \sigma^2_\delta\) are the discrepancy GP posterior moments. The two GPs are trained independently: first the low-fidelity GP on the full cheap dataset, then the discrepancy GP on \(\delta_i = y_H(\mathbf{x}_i) - \rho\,f_L(\mathbf{x}_i)\) at the expensive design sites.

In a BLISS-2026 context:

Level Cost Use
\(f_L\) Analytical surrogate (ms/call) LHS initial DOE, EI candidates
\(f_M\) Medium CFD (minutes/call) Confirm promising regions
\(f_H\) Full RANS + FEM (hours/call) Final design validation only

12.3 Neural Network Surrogates

For black-box responses with strong nonlinearity, local optima, or discontinuities — common in structural crashworthiness or combustion instability — neither quadratic polynomials nor stationary GP kernels are adequate. Neural network surrogates (also called metamodels or emulators) can represent essentially arbitrary smooth functions given sufficient data.

A feedforward network with \(L\) layers:

\[ \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) = W_L \sigma(\cdots W_2 \sigma(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1) + b_2 \cdots) + b_L \tag{12.4}\]

For the BLISS-2026 surrogate role — 20–120 BB evaluations per outer iteration, dimensions 5–15, engineering responses that are generally smooth — GPs are the right default. Neural networks become relevant when the response has sharp pressure spikes near resonance, or when a large archive of legacy simulation data can pre-train the network before the BLISS outer loop begins.

12.4 Physics-Informed Neural Networks

A key limitation of purely data-driven surrogates — whether GP or NN — is that they ignore the physics. A GP fitted to 20 SRM range evaluations has no knowledge that mass must be conserved, that chamber pressure obeys Saint-Robert’s law, or that thrust cannot be negative. Violating these constraints in extrapolation produces physically impossible predictions.

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) [3] address this by adding residuals of the governing equations directly to the training loss. For a system governed by a PDE \(\mathcal{N}[u] = 0\), the PINN loss is:

\[ \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}}_{\text{fit observations}} + \lambda\,\underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}}_{\text{satisfy physics}} \tag{12.5}\]

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{i=1}^{N_c} \left|\mathcal{N}[\hat{u}](\mathbf{x}_i^c)\right|^2 \tag{12.6}\]

where \(\mathbf{x}_i^c\) are collocation points (not necessarily observed data), and \(\hat{u}\) is the neural network approximation. Automatic differentiation computes \(\mathcal{N}[\hat{u}]\) — derivatives through the network — analytically.

12.4.1 PINN for the SRM Chamber Pressure ODE

The quasi-steady SRM chamber pressure is governed by:

\[ \frac{dP_c}{dt} = \frac{c^* \rho_p a A_b P_c^n - P_c A_t}{V_c / c^*} \tag{12.7}\]

A PINN surrogate for \(P_c(t)\) encodes Equation 12.7 as the physics residual, so even with sparse static fire measurements it cannot predict non-monotone pressure build-up or negative chamber pressures during the quench transient.

Code 
# Simulate 'true' SRM Pc(t) via simple ODE integration
function pc_ode(t_max; Pc0=14.7, a=0.038, n=0.35, Ab=800.0, At=5.0,
                c_star=4900.0, rho_p=0.064, V0=3000.0)
    Pc = Pc0
    dt = 0.05
    ts = [0.0]; Pcs = [Pc]
    Vweb = V0
    for _ in 0:dt:t_max-dt
        r    = a * Pc^n
        mdot = rho_p * Ab * r
        Pc   = (mdot * c_star) / At
        Pc   = max(Pc, 14.7)
        Vweb -= Ab * r * dt
        (Vweb <= 0) && break
        push!(ts, ts[end]+dt); push!(Pcs, Pc)
    end
    push!(ts, ts[end]+dt); push!(Pcs, 14.7)
    ts, Pcs
end

t_true, Pc_true = pc_ode(20.0)

# 6 sparse observations
obs_idx = round.(Int, range(1, length(t_true)-2, length=6))
t_obs = t_true[obs_idx]
Pc_obs = Pc_true[obs_idx]

# Plain GP on the observations
t_lo, t_hi = 0.0, 22.0
tc_obs  = (t_obs  .- t_lo) ./ (t_hi - t_lo)
tc_pred = collect(range(0.0, 1.0, length=200))

μ_pc, σ²_pc = predict_gp_scaled(gp_pc, ym_pc, ys_pc,
                                  reshape(tc_pred, :, 1))
σ_pc = sqrt.(max.(σ²_pc, 0.0))
t_pred_nat = t_lo .+ tc_pred .* (t_hi - t_lo)

# PINN: enforce Pc >= 14.7 and monotone tail-off using physics prior
# Approximate as: PINN ≈ GP mean clipped to physical bounds + smooth tail
burnout_t = t_true[end-1]
bc_idx    = findfirst(>=(burnout_t), t_pred_nat)
isnothing(bc_idx) && (bc_idx = length(t_pred_nat))
μ_pinn    = copy(μ_pc)
# After burnout: exponential decay to ambient
for i in bc_idx:length(μ_pinn)
    τ = t_pred_nat[i] - burnout_t
    μ_pinn[i] = 14.7 + max(μ_pc[bc_idx] - 14.7, 0.0) * exp(-τ * 0.8)
end
μ_pinn = max.(μ_pinn, 14.7)   # physics: Pc ≥ P_ambient

p = plot(t_true, Pc_true, lw=2, color=:black, label="True Pc(t)")
plot!(t_pred_nat, μ_pc, lw=2, color=:crimson, linestyle=:dash, label="GP mean")
plot!(t_pred_nat, max.(μ_pc .- 2σ_pc, 0.0),
      fillrange=μ_pc .+ 2σ_pc, fillalpha=0.15, color=:crimson, lw=0, label="GP ±2σ")
plot!(t_pred_nat, μ_pinn, lw=2, color=:steelblue, label="PINN (physics-constrained)")
scatter!(t_obs, Pc_obs, color=:black, markersize=7, markershape=:star5,
         markerstrokecolor=:white, label="Observations (6 pts)")
hline!([14.7], color=:gray, lw=1, linestyle=:dot, label="Ambient pressure")
plot!(xlabel="Time (s)", ylabel="Chamber pressure Pc (psia)",
      title="PINN vs. GP: SRM Chamber Pressure",

PINNs are most valuable when:

  1. Observations are very sparse relative to the response complexity
  2. The governing equations are known (even approximately)
  3. Physical constraint violations carry engineering consequences — negative pressure, imaginary velocities, non-positive-definite stress tensors

They are not always better: PINNs require differentiating the network through the PDE residual, which adds training complexity. For smooth, well-sampled responses, a plain GP remains faster to fit and easier to interpret.

12.5 Automatic Differentiation in Julia

BLISS-RS and BLISS-2026 both treat disciplines as black boxes — no gradients required. This is deliberate: many legacy analysis codes cannot be differentiated. But when the black box is differentiable, providing exact gradients to the system optimizer can dramatically reduce BB evaluation counts.

Julia’s automatic differentiation ecosystem makes this practical:

Package Approach Best for
ForwardDiff.jl Forward-mode AD Low \(n\), cheap-per-call BBs
ReverseDiff.jl Reverse-mode AD High \(n\), scalar output
Zygote.jl Source-to-source reverse Flux/ML pipelines
Enzyme.jl LLVM-level AD Maximum performance

Forward-mode computes the full Jacobian in \(n\) passes (one per input). Reverse-mode computes the full gradient in one pass regardless of \(n\) — critical for \(n \gg 1\).

12.5.1 AD-Augmented BLISS

When gradients are available, the EI inner loop in BLISS-2026 can be replaced by a gradient-based optimizer, reducing the number of EI evaluations from hundreds to tens. The trust-region update can also use gradient information to set tighter, more accurate bounds — anisotropic shrinkage aligned with the response curvature rather than the isotropic \(K\)-factor used in BLISS-RS.

The sensitivity vector \(\nabla_Z f(\mathbf{Z}^*)\) also provides a direct ranking of system variables by importance, complementing the ARD length scales from the GP kernel.

12.6 Uncertainty Quantification Propagation

BLISS-2026’s GP surrogates do not just improve prediction accuracy — they enable systematic uncertainty propagation through the MDO system. When the GP posterior \(p(f(\mathbf{Z}) \mid \text{data})\) is a full distribution rather than a point estimate, we can ask: what is the probability distribution over the optimal design?

12.6.2 Robust Optimisation

The standard BLISS-2026 objective maximises expected range: \(\max_Z \mu(\mathbf{Z})\). A robust variant instead maximises:

\[ \max_{\mathbf{Z}} \left[\mu(\mathbf{Z}) - \lambda\,\sigma(\mathbf{Z})\right] \tag{12.8}\]

where \(\lambda > 0\) is a risk-aversion parameter. At \(\lambda = 0\) this recovers the standard problem; as \(\lambda \to \infty\) the optimizer avoids uncertain regions entirely, preferring well-sampled designs even at lower expected performance. In practice \(\lambda = 1\) to \(2\) gives a good balance.

12.7 Digital Twins and BLISS

A digital twin is a continuously-updated, high-fidelity computational model of a physical asset, synchronized with real-world sensor data. For an SRM test program, a digital twin might consist of:

  • A combustion CFD model updated after each static fire with measured \(P_c(t)\), \(F(t)\)
  • A structural finite element model updated with strain gauge data
  • A coupled aeroelastic model tracking thermal erosion of the nozzle throat

BLISS-2026 maps naturally onto the digital-twin architecture:

In this closed loop:

  • After each physical test, the measured performance data updates the GP surrogate as new training observations (sequential Bayesian updating).
  • The system optimizer re-runs with the updated posterior, identifying the next most informative design to test.
  • The process terminates when the GP posterior variance is below a threshold — the design has been identified with confidence.

This is the Bayesian experimental design paradigm applied to BLISS, and it is the natural long-term direction of the framework developed in this book.

12.8 Reinforcement Learning for MDO

An emerging research direction treats MDO as a sequential decision problem and applies reinforcement learning (RL) [4]. The optimizer is an RL agent that learns a policy \(\pi(a \mid s)\) mapping the current design state \(s\) (observed BB outputs, interval bounds, GP uncertainty map) to a design action \(a\) (next \(\mathbf{Z}\) to evaluate).

Unlike Bayesian optimization — which must solve a global EI maximisation at every step — an RL policy evaluates in microseconds once trained. The training cost is paid offline across thousands of synthetic MDO problems; the inference cost at deployment is negligible.

12.8.1 Framing BLISS as an RL Problem

RL concept BLISS-2026 equivalent
State \(s_t\) GP posterior \(({\boldsymbol\mu}_t, {\boldsymbol\sigma}_t)\), current bounds \(\mathcal{D}_t\), best \(f^*_t\)
Action \(a_t\) Next design \(\mathbf{Z}_{t+1}\) to evaluate
Reward \(r_t\) \(f(\mathbf{Z}_{t+1}) - f^*_t\) (improvement on best)
Episode end Budget exhausted or \(\sigma^2 < \varepsilon\) everywhere
Policy \(\pi\) Neural network mapping state → action

The agent learns, across many training episodes on synthetic test functions, to balance exploration (high \(\sigma\)) against exploitation (high \(\mu\)) — exactly the EI trade-off, but encoded implicitly in the network weights rather than solved analytically at each step.

# Simplified 1-D Bayesian bandit: compare EI vs. random policy on 15 evaluations
# Demonstrates the RL framing — a learned policy would replace the EI maximiser

function bandit_trial(f_true, lo, hi, n_obs; policy=:ei, seed=42)
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)
    # Seed with 3 random points
    x_obs = sort(lo .+ rand(rng, 3) .* (hi - lo))
    y_obs = f_true.(x_obs)
    regrets = Float64[]
    f_global = maximum(f_true.(range(lo, hi, length=500)))

    for _ in 1:(n_obs - 3)
        xc = (x_obs .- lo) ./ (hi - lo)
        gp, ym, ys = fit_gp_auto(reshape(xc, :, 1), y_obs)
        cands = range(0.0, 1.0, length=200)
        X_cand = reshape(collect(cands), :, 1)
        μ_c, σ²_c = predict_gp_scaled(gp, ym, ys, X_cand)
        σ_c = sqrt.(max.(σ²_c, 0.0))

        x_next_c = if policy == :ei
            f_best = maximum(y_obs)
            ei = expected_improvement(μ_c, σ²_c, f_best; ξ=1.0)
            cands[argmax(ei)]
        else
            rand(rng)
        end
        x_next = lo + x_next_c * (hi - lo)
        push!(x_obs, x_next)
        push!(y_obs, f_true(x_next))
        push!(regrets, f_global - maximum(y_obs))
    end
    regrets
end

f1d(x) = sin(5x) * exp(-0.5x) + 0.3*cos(9x)
r_ei  = bandit_trial(f1d, 0.0, 3.0, 15; policy=:ei,     seed=7)
r_rnd = bandit_trial(f1d, 0.0, 3.0, 15; policy=:random, seed=7)

plot(r_ei,  lw=2, color=:steelblue, marker=:circle, markersize=5,
     label="EI policy (Bayesian)", xlabel="Evaluation number (after seed)",
     ylabel="Simple regret", title="EI vs. Random: 1-D Bandit",
     legend=:topright, grid=true, gridalpha=0.3, yscale=:log10)
plot!(r_rnd, lw=2, color=:crimson, marker=:circle, markersize=5,
      linestyle=:dash, label="Random policy")

Early results suggest RL-based MDO can match or exceed EI-based BO on moderate- dimensional problems (\(n \leq 20\)) with an order-of-magnitude reduction in online computation. This is an active research frontier as of 2026.

12.9 Modern MDO Software Frameworks

BLISS-2026 is a research implementation in Julia, purpose-built to expose every algorithmic detail. Production MDO work typically uses one of several mature frameworks that handle the scaffolding — derivative propagation, discipline connections, parallel execution — allowing engineers to focus on discipline models.

12.9.1 OpenMDAO

OpenMDAO [5] is the most widely used open-source MDO framework. Developed at NASA Glenn Research Center, it implements MDF, IDF, and SAND architectures with unified derivative propagation via the unified derivatives framework (UDF). Disciplines are implemented as ExplicitComponent or ImplicitComponent objects; OpenMDAO assembles the system Jacobian and passes it to gradient-based optimizers (SNOPT, IPOPT, SLSQP).

OpenMDAO handles what BLISS-2026 does not: analytic derivatives, large-scale sparse Jacobians, and integration with CFD codes via the OpenAeroStruct and CCBlade discipline libraries.

12.9.2 Where to Use Each

Scenario Recommended Tool
Black-box disciplines, no source access BLISS-2026 (this book)
Disciplines in Python with analytic derivs OpenMDAO
Trajectory optimization, dynamics Dymos (on top of OpenMDAO)
Large-scale aerostruct (wing box + aero) OpenAeroStruct
Prototype research, full algorithmic control Julia (this book’s approach)

The frameworks are complementary: OpenMDAO excels at gradient-based MDO over differentiable disciplines; BLISS-2026 excels at surrogate-based MDO over legacy black boxes. A multi-fidelity workflow might use BLISS-2026 for the outer surrogate loop and OpenMDAO for the inner high-fidelity validation calls.

This chapter placed BLISS-2026 within the broader MDO landscape and identified the directions most likely to matter in practice:

  • Decomposition architectures — MDF, IDF, SAND, CO, ATC, and BLISS occupy different positions on the monolithic-vs.-decomposed and gradient-vs.-surrogate axes. BLISS is the natural choice for black-box disciplines.

  • RL for MDO is an emerging frontier: a trained policy replaces the per-step EI maximisation, carrying negligible online cost after offline training.

### MDF, IDF, and SAND

**MDF (Multidisciplinary Feasible)** enforces disciplinary consistency at every
system analysis call. Couplings are satisfied implicitly by a fixed-point (Gauss-Seidel)
or Newton iteration. The system optimizer sees only converged analyses but pays the
cost of full convergence each function evaluation.

**IDF (Individual Discipline Feasible)** introduces explicit coupling variables as
optimization unknowns. Disciplines run independently without iteration, and coupling
consistency is enforced by the outer optimizer as equality constraints. This enables
parallelism at the expense of a larger (but well-structured) optimization problem.

**SAND (Simultaneous Analysis and Design)** treats all state and design variables as
optimization variables simultaneously. The optimizer solves the governing equations
and the design problem jointly. Extremely fast when it converges — but the problem
is large and requires derivatives of the governing equations.

### Collaborative Optimisation and ATC

**Collaborative Optimisation (CO)** [@alexandrov2001approximation] places a separate
optimizer at each discipline level that minimises the deviation between local design
variables and the system-level targets. The system-level optimizer drives coupling
consistency. CO handles large numbers of disciplines but can exhibit convergence
difficulties when coupling is strong.

**Analytical Target Cascading (ATC)** propagates design targets hierarchically from
the system level down through subsystem and component levels, with augmented Lagrangian
penalties enforcing consistency. It is well-suited to hierarchical product architectures
(vehicle → subsystem → component) where the coupling structure mirrors the bill of
materials.

### Where BLISS Sits

BLISS is a two-level decomposition that inherits IDF's independent disciplinary
structure but adds the distinction between system-level ($Z$) and local ($X$)
variables. The key innovation is that the system optimizer never calls the
disciplines directly — it operates entirely on surrogate models. This makes BLISS
uniquely suited to black-box disciplines: codes that cannot be differentiated or
whose source is unavailable.

## Multi-Fidelity Methods

The most impactful practical extension to BLISS-2026 is **multi-fidelity** modeling:
using a hierarchy of analysis tools of differing fidelity and cost to accelerate
convergence.

### Kennedy-O'Hagan Co-Kriging

Kennedy and O'Hagan [@kennedy2000predicting] proposed a principled Bayesian
framework for combining models at multiple fidelity levels. For two levels (cheap
$f_L$ and expensive $f_H$):

$$
f_H(\mathbf{x}) = \rho \cdot f_L(\mathbf{x}) + \delta(\mathbf{x})
$$ {#eq-cokriging}

where $\rho$ is a regression scaling coefficient and $\delta(\mathbf{x})$ is an
independent GP capturing the discrepancy between the two fidelity levels. The co-kriging
posterior combines information from many cheap runs and few expensive runs.

The co-kriging posterior mean and variance at a test point $\mathbf{x}_*$ are:

$$
\mu_{CK}(\mathbf{x}_*) = \rho\,\mu_L(\mathbf{x}_*) + \mu_\delta(\mathbf{x}_*)
$$ {#eq-cokriging-mean}

$$
\sigma^2_{CK}(\mathbf{x}_*) = \rho^2\,\sigma^2_L(\mathbf{x}_*) + \sigma^2_\delta(\mathbf{x}_*)
$$ {#eq-cokriging-var}

where $\mu_L, \sigma^2_L$ are the low-fidelity GP posterior moments and
$\mu_\delta, \sigma^2_\delta$ are the discrepancy GP posterior moments. The two
GPs are trained independently: first the low-fidelity GP on the full cheap dataset,
then the discrepancy GP on $\delta_i = y_H(\mathbf{x}_i) - \rho\,f_L(\mathbf{x}_i)$
at the expensive design sites.

In a BLISS-2026 context:

| Level | Cost | Use |
|-------|------|-----|
| $f_L$ | Analytical surrogate (ms/call) | LHS initial DOE, EI candidates |
| $f_M$ | Medium CFD (minutes/call) | Confirm promising regions |
| $f_H$ | Full RANS + FEM (hours/call) | Final design validation only |

## Neural Network Surrogates

For black-box responses with strong nonlinearity, local optima, or discontinuities —
common in structural crashworthiness or combustion instability — neither quadratic
polynomials nor stationary GP kernels are adequate. **Neural network surrogates**
(also called metamodels or emulators) can represent essentially arbitrary smooth
functions given sufficient data.

A feedforward network with $L$ layers:

$$
\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) = W_L \sigma(\cdots W_2 \sigma(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1) + b_2 \cdots) + b_L
$$ {#eq-nn}

### Julia Implementation (from scratch, no Flux dependency)

```{julia}
#| label: lst-nn-demo
#| code-fold: false

struct TwoLayerNet
    W1::Matrix{Float64}; b1::Vector{Float64}
    W2::Matrix{Float64}; b2::Vector{Float64}
    W3::Matrix{Float64}; b3::Vector{Float64}
end

relu(x) = max.(x, 0.0)

function forward(net::TwoLayerNet, X::Matrix{Float64})
    h1 = relu.(net.W1 * X' .+ net.b1)
    h2 = relu.(net.W2 * h1 .+ net.b2)
    ŷ  = net.W3 * h2 .+ net.b3
    return vec(ŷ)
end

function init_net(n_in::Int; h=16, seed=42)
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)
    s1, s2 = sqrt(2/n_in), sqrt(2/h)
    TwoLayerNet(
        randn(rng, h, n_in) * s1, zeros(h),
        randn(rng, h, h)    * s2, zeros(h),
        randn(rng, 1, h)    * s2, zeros(1),
    )
end

function train_nn(net::TwoLayerNet, X_train, y_train;
                  n_epochs=500, lr=0.05, seed=42)
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)
    function net_to_vec(n)
        vcat(vec(n.W1), n.b1, vec(n.W2), n.b2, vec(n.W3), n.b3)
    end
    function vec_to_net(v, n)
        h, ni = size(n.W1)
        i = 0
        W1 = reshape(v[i+1:i+h*ni], h, ni); i += h*ni
        b1 = v[i+1:i+h];  i += h
        W2 = reshape(v[i+1:i+h*h], h, h);  i += h*h
        b2 = v[i+1:i+h];  i += h
        W3 = reshape(v[i+1:i+h], 1, h);    i += h
        b3 = v[i+1:i+1];
        TwoLayerNet(W1,b1,W2,b2,W3,b3)
    end
    mse(n) = mean((forward(n, X_train) .- y_train).^2)
    θ = net_to_vec(net)
    for _ in 1:n_epochs
        g = similar(θ)
        f0 = mse(vec_to_net(θ, net))
        ε  = lr * sqrt(f0 + 1e-8)
        for j in eachindex(θ)
            θ[j] += ε
            g[j]  = (mse(vec_to_net(θ, net)) - f0) / ε
            θ[j] -= ε
        end
        θ .-= lr * g
    end
    return vec_to_net(θ, net)
end

rng4   = MersenneTwister(77)
n_demo = 100
X_demo = rand(rng4, n_demo, 2) .* 4 .- 2
y_demo2= [100*(X_demo[i,2]-X_demo[i,1]^2)^2 + (X_demo[i,1]-1)^2
          for i in 1:n_demo]

net0    = init_net(2)
net_fit = train_nn(net0, X_demo, y_demo2; n_epochs=300, lr=0.005)

X_test4 = rand(rng4, 50, 2) .* 4 .- 2
y_test4 = [100*(X_test4[i,2]-X_test4[i,1]^2)^2 + (X_test4[i,1]-1)^2
           for i in 1:50]
y_pred4 = forward(net_fit, X_test4)
@printf("NN surrogate RMSE on Rosenbrock: %.1f\n",
        sqrt(mean((y_test4 .- y_pred4).^2)))
```

| Criterion | Use GP | Use NN |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Training data budget | < 500 points | > 500 points |
| Dimension $n$ | ≤ 30 | Any |
| Uncertainty needed | Yes | Approximate (MC dropout) |
| Response smoothness | Smooth, unimodal | Rough, multimodal, discontinuous |
| Derivative needed | Analytic | Autodiff (ForwardDiff.jl) |

For the BLISS-2026 surrogate role — 20–120 BB evaluations per outer iteration,
dimensions 5–15, engineering responses that are generally smooth — GPs are the
right default. Neural networks become relevant when the response has sharp
pressure spikes near resonance, or when a large archive of legacy simulation data
can pre-train the network before the BLISS outer loop begins.

## Physics-Informed Neural Networks

A key limitation of purely data-driven surrogates — whether GP or NN — is that they
ignore the physics. A GP fitted to 20 SRM range evaluations has no knowledge that
mass must be conserved, that chamber pressure obeys Saint-Robert's law, or that
thrust cannot be negative. Violating these constraints in extrapolation produces
physically impossible predictions.

**Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)** [@raissi2019physics] address this by
adding residuals of the governing equations directly to the training loss. For a
system governed by a PDE $\mathcal{N}[u] = 0$, the PINN loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}}_{\text{fit observations}}
            + \lambda\,\underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}}_{\text{satisfy physics}}
$$ {#eq-pinn-loss}

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{i=1}^{N_c} \left|\mathcal{N}[\hat{u}](\mathbf{x}_i^c)\right|^2
$$ {#eq-pinn-pde}

where $\mathbf{x}_i^c$ are collocation points (not necessarily observed data), and
$\hat{u}$ is the neural network approximation. Automatic differentiation computes
$\mathcal{N}[\hat{u}]$ — derivatives through the network — analytically.

### PINN for the SRM Chamber Pressure ODE

The quasi-steady SRM chamber pressure is governed by:

$$
\frac{dP_c}{dt} = \frac{c^* \rho_p a A_b P_c^n - P_c A_t}{V_c / c^*}
$$ {#eq-pc-ode}

A PINN surrogate for $P_c(t)$ encodes @eq-pc-ode as the physics residual, so even
with sparse static fire measurements it cannot predict non-monotone pressure build-up
or negative chamber pressures during the quench transient.

```{julia}

function pc_ode(t_max; Pc0=14.7, a=0.038, n=0.35, Ab=800.0, At=5.0,
                c_star=4900.0, rho_p=0.064, V0=3000.0)
    Pc = Pc0
    dt = 0.05
    ts = [0.0]; Pcs = [Pc]
    Vweb = V0
    for _ in 0:dt:t_max-dt
        r    = a * Pc^n
        mdot = rho_p * Ab * r
        Pc   = (mdot * c_star) / At
        Pc   = max(Pc, 14.7)
        Vweb -= Ab * r * dt
        (Vweb <= 0) && break
        push!(ts, ts[end]+dt); push!(Pcs, Pc)
    end
    push!(ts, ts[end]+dt); push!(Pcs, 14.7)
    ts, Pcs
end

isnothing(bc_idx) && (bc_idx = length(t_pred_nat))
```

PINNs are most valuable when:

1. Observations are very sparse relative to the response complexity
2. The governing equations are known (even approximately)
3. Physical constraint violations carry engineering consequences — negative pressure,
   imaginary velocities, non-positive-definite stress tensors

They are not always better: PINNs require differentiating the network through the
PDE residual, which adds training complexity. For smooth, well-sampled responses,
a plain GP remains faster to fit and easier to interpret.

## Automatic Differentiation in Julia

BLISS-RS and BLISS-2026 both treat disciplines as black boxes — no gradients
required. This is deliberate: many legacy analysis codes cannot be differentiated.
But when the black box *is* differentiable, providing exact gradients to the
system optimizer can dramatically reduce BB evaluation counts.

Julia's automatic differentiation ecosystem makes this practical:

| Package | Approach | Best for |
|---------|----------|----------|
| `ForwardDiff.jl` | Forward-mode AD | Low $n$, cheap-per-call BBs |
| `ReverseDiff.jl` | Reverse-mode AD | High $n$, scalar output |
| `Zygote.jl` | Source-to-source reverse | Flux/ML pipelines |
| `Enzyme.jl` | LLVM-level AD | Maximum performance |

Forward-mode computes the full Jacobian in $n$ passes (one per input). Reverse-mode
computes the full gradient in one pass regardless of $n$ — critical for $n \gg 1$.

### AD-Augmented BLISS

When gradients are available, the EI inner loop in BLISS-2026 can be replaced by a
gradient-based optimizer, reducing the number of EI evaluations from hundreds to
tens. The trust-region update can also use gradient information to set tighter,
more accurate bounds — anisotropic shrinkage aligned with the response curvature
rather than the isotropic $K$-factor used in BLISS-RS.

The sensitivity vector $\nabla_Z f(\mathbf{Z}^*)$ also provides a direct ranking
of system variables by importance, complementing the ARD length scales from the
GP kernel.

## Uncertainty Quantification Propagation

BLISS-2026's GP surrogates do not just improve prediction accuracy — they enable
systematic **uncertainty propagation** through the MDO system. When the GP posterior
$p(f(\mathbf{Z}) \mid \text{data})$ is a full distribution rather than a point
estimate, we can ask: what is the probability distribution over the optimal design?

### Robust Optimisation

The standard BLISS-2026 objective maximises expected range:
$\max_Z \mu(\mathbf{Z})$. A *robust* variant instead maximises:

$$
\max_{\mathbf{Z}} \left[\mu(\mathbf{Z}) - \lambda\,\sigma(\mathbf{Z})\right]
$$ {#eq-robust-obj}

where $\lambda > 0$ is a risk-aversion parameter. At $\lambda = 0$ this recovers
the standard problem; as $\lambda \to \infty$ the optimizer avoids uncertain regions
entirely, preferring well-sampled designs even at lower expected performance. In
practice $\lambda = 1$ to $2$ gives a good balance.

## Digital Twins and BLISS

A **digital twin** is a continuously-updated, high-fidelity computational model of
a physical asset, synchronized with real-world sensor data. For an SRM test program,
a digital twin might consist of:

- A combustion CFD model updated after each static fire with measured $P_c(t)$, $F(t)$
- A structural finite element model updated with strain gauge data
- A coupled aeroelastic model tracking thermal erosion of the nozzle throat

In this closed loop:

- After each physical test, the measured performance data updates the GP surrogate
  as new training observations (sequential Bayesian updating).
- The system optimizer re-runs with the updated posterior, identifying the next
  most informative design to test.
- The process terminates when the GP posterior variance is below a threshold —
  the design has been identified with confidence.

This is the **Bayesian experimental design** paradigm applied to BLISS, and it is
the natural long-term direction of the framework developed in this book.

## Reinforcement Learning for MDO

An emerging research direction treats MDO as a **sequential decision problem** and
applies reinforcement learning (RL) [@sutton2018reinforcement]. The optimizer is an
RL agent that learns a policy $\pi(a \mid s)$ mapping the current design state $s$
(observed BB outputs, interval bounds, GP uncertainty map) to a design action $a$
(next $\mathbf{Z}$ to evaluate).

Unlike Bayesian optimization — which must solve a global EI maximisation at every
step — an RL policy evaluates in microseconds once trained. The training cost is
paid offline across thousands of synthetic MDO problems; the inference cost at
deployment is negligible.

### Framing BLISS as an RL Problem

| RL concept | BLISS-2026 equivalent |
|------------|----------------------|
| State $s_t$ | GP posterior $({\boldsymbol\mu}_t, {\boldsymbol\sigma}_t)$, current bounds $\mathcal{D}_t$, best $f^*_t$ |
| Action $a_t$ | Next design $\mathbf{Z}_{t+1}$ to evaluate |
| Reward $r_t$ | $f(\mathbf{Z}_{t+1}) - f^*_t$ (improvement on best) |
| Episode end | Budget exhausted or $\sigma^2 < \varepsilon$ everywhere |
| Policy $\pi$ | Neural network mapping state → action |

The agent learns, across many training episodes on synthetic test functions, to
balance exploration (high $\sigma$) against exploitation (high $\mu$) — exactly
the EI trade-off, but encoded implicitly in the network weights rather than solved
analytically at each step.

```{julia}
#| label: lst-rl-bandit
#| code-fold: false

# Simplified 1-D Bayesian bandit: compare EI vs. random policy on 15 evaluations
# Demonstrates the RL framing — a learned policy would replace the EI maximiser

function bandit_trial(f_true, lo, hi, n_obs; policy=:ei, seed=42)
    rng = MersenneTwister(seed)
    # Seed with 3 random points
    x_obs = sort(lo .+ rand(rng, 3) .* (hi - lo))
    y_obs = f_true.(x_obs)
    regrets = Float64[]
    f_global = maximum(f_true.(range(lo, hi, length=500)))

    for _ in 1:(n_obs - 3)
        xc = (x_obs .- lo) ./ (hi - lo)
        gp, ym, ys = fit_gp_auto(reshape(xc, :, 1), y_obs)
        cands = range(0.0, 1.0, length=200)
        X_cand = reshape(collect(cands), :, 1)
        μ_c, σ²_c = predict_gp_scaled(gp, ym, ys, X_cand)
        σ_c = sqrt.(max.(σ²_c, 0.0))

        x_next_c = if policy == :ei
            f_best = maximum(y_obs)
            ei = expected_improvement(μ_c, σ²_c, f_best; ξ=1.0)
            cands[argmax(ei)]
        else
            rand(rng)
        end
        x_next = lo + x_next_c * (hi - lo)
        push!(x_obs, x_next)
        push!(y_obs, f_true(x_next))
        push!(regrets, f_global - maximum(y_obs))
    end
    regrets
end

f1d(x) = sin(5x) * exp(-0.5x) + 0.3*cos(9x)
r_ei  = bandit_trial(f1d, 0.0, 3.0, 15; policy=:ei,     seed=7)
r_rnd = bandit_trial(f1d, 0.0, 3.0, 15; policy=:random, seed=7)

plot(r_ei,  lw=2, color=:steelblue, marker=:circle, markersize=5,
     label="EI policy (Bayesian)", xlabel="Evaluation number (after seed)",
     ylabel="Simple regret", title="EI vs. Random: 1-D Bandit",
     legend=:topright, grid=true, gridalpha=0.3, yscale=:log10)
plot!(r_rnd, lw=2, color=:crimson, marker=:circle, markersize=5,
      linestyle=:dash, label="Random policy")
```

Early results suggest RL-based MDO can match or exceed EI-based BO on moderate-
dimensional problems ($n \leq 20$) with an order-of-magnitude reduction in
online computation. This is an active research frontier as of 2026.

## Modern MDO Software Frameworks

BLISS-2026 is a research implementation in Julia, purpose-built to expose every
algorithmic detail. Production MDO work typically uses one of several mature
frameworks that handle the scaffolding — derivative propagation, discipline
connections, parallel execution — allowing engineers to focus on discipline models.

### OpenMDAO

**OpenMDAO** [@gray2019openmdao] is the most widely used open-source MDO framework.
Developed at NASA Glenn Research Center, it implements MDF, IDF, and SAND
architectures with unified derivative propagation via the **unified derivatives
framework** (UDF). Disciplines are implemented as `ExplicitComponent` or
`ImplicitComponent` objects; OpenMDAO assembles the system Jacobian and passes it
to gradient-based optimizers (SNOPT, IPOPT, SLSQP).

OpenMDAO handles what BLISS-2026 does not: analytic derivatives, large-scale sparse
Jacobians, and integration with CFD codes via the `OpenAeroStruct` and `CCBlade`
discipline libraries.

### Where to Use Each

| Scenario | Recommended Tool |
|----------|-----------------|
| Black-box disciplines, no source access | BLISS-2026 (this book) |
| Disciplines in Python with analytic derivs | OpenMDAO |
| Trajectory optimization, dynamics | Dymos (on top of OpenMDAO) |
| Large-scale aerostruct (wing box + aero) | OpenAeroStruct |
| Prototype research, full algorithmic control | Julia (this book's approach) |

The frameworks are complementary: OpenMDAO excels at gradient-based MDO over
differentiable disciplines; BLISS-2026 excels at surrogate-based MDO over legacy
black boxes. A multi-fidelity workflow might use BLISS-2026 for the outer surrogate
loop and OpenMDAO for the inner high-fidelity validation calls.

## Summary: Where MDO Is Heading

| Trend | Impact on BLISS-2026 |
|-------|---------------------|
| **GP + EI** (this book) | Better surrogates, adaptive sampling, UQ |
| **Multi-fidelity co-kriging** | 10–100× cheaper per iteration |
| **Neural network surrogates** | Handle rough, high-dim responses |
| **Physics-informed NNs** | Encode governing equations in surrogate loss |
| **Automatic differentiation** | Exact gradients through any Julia BB |
| **Digital twins** | Online GP updates from sensor data |
| **Robust optimization** | Designs robust to model uncertainty |
| **RL-based MDO** | Policy networks replace EI solver |

The thread connecting all of these is the Bayesian perspective: rather than returning
a single point estimate, the surrogate returns a *probability distribution* over
possible function values. This distribution drives every downstream decision —
which point to evaluate next, whether to trust the current optimum, how robust the
design is to uncertainty. BLISS-2026 established the GP as the core surrogate in
this framework; the extensions above are natural continuations of the same idea.

## Summary

This chapter placed BLISS-2026 within the broader MDO landscape and identified the
directions most likely to matter in practice:

- **Decomposition architectures** — MDF, IDF, SAND, CO, ATC, and BLISS occupy
  different positions on the monolithic-vs.-decomposed and gradient-vs.-surrogate
  axes. BLISS is the natural choice for black-box disciplines.
- **Multi-fidelity co-kriging** extends the GP surrogate to exploit cheap
  approximations, potentially reducing expensive BB calls by an order of magnitude.
- **Neural network surrogates** complement GPs for rough, high-dimensional, or
  data-rich responses; PINNs further constrain networks with governing equations
  to prevent physically impossible extrapolation.
- **Automatic differentiation** in Julia makes exact gradient computation trivial
  for differentiable BBs, enabling gradient-augmented trust-region updates.
- **Digital twins** and online Bayesian updating close the loop between physical
  test data and the surrogate model, turning BLISS into a continuously learning
  system rather than a one-shot optimizer.
- **RL for MDO** is an emerging frontier: a trained policy replaces the per-step
  EI maximisation, carrying negligible online cost after offline training.
